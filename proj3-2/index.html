In this project we added on to the first part of project three by adding in more complex visual components. The extra visual components we chose were part 1 - rendering mirror and glass surfaces with reflection and refraction -  and part 4 - enabling a depth of field effect by simulating a lens instead of a pinhole camera. In part 1, we started by implementing the mirror effects with reflection. We ran into trouble when we then implemented the refraction effect. For a while, the glass ball was rendered as a black sphere. We resolved this issue by changing the program arguments to ensure there are enough bounces for the rays of light to refract through the glass ball. This value is at least 3 bounces to see the full effects. It also helped us understand how the refraction is happening through the ball. In part 4, we started by fully understanding the principles of a lens. Our biggest roadblock in this part was understanding the physics well enough to translate this into code. Once we were able to understand the physics of the plane of focus with respect to the lens, we were able to successfully implement the depth of field effect.

  • In a few sentences, explain the differences between a pinhole camera model and a thin-lens camera model.

A pinhole camera model has no lens and a tiny aperture.  This means that everything is in focus, regardless of focalDistance. This is because there is no lens. In a thins-lens camera model, the aperture is a finite number defined by lens radius. Objects are only in focus in the camera model if they’re within the plane that is located at focalDistance from the lens object.
